Strategic Insight

The Big Realization

Users weren't buying software—they were buying human support

BrightBid was selling itself as a self-service AI tool for managing Google Ads. But after talking to 50+ users, the research showed something different: users weren't buying the software. They were buying access to a human—the digital specialist assigned to their account. The tool was just a nice-to-have.

This insight pointed toward a different business model. About 18 months later, under new leadership, the company moved toward an agency model—aligning with what the research had suggested.

Insights & Outcomes

What I Found and What I Did About It

1

Users don't trust the AI

Evidence: Interviews and analytics showed low adoption of automated features

What I designed: A multi-layered approach to building trust—show the AI's reasoning, suggest changes instead of applying them automatically, give users progressive control (from suggestion to approval to automation), and always show an "undo" option

Users reported being more willing to try automation features. Adoption rates improved.
2

Digital specialists need dashboards they can customize per client

Evidence: Every specialist had completely different workflows

What I designed: Widget-based dashboards with drag-and-drop customization and filtering

Specialists could finally tailor views to each client's needs without workarounds
3

The campaign creation flow was too fragmented

Evidence: Specialists were context-switching between Google Ads and BrightBid constantly

What I designed: Unified flow with AI assistance baked in, based on analytics showing which fields took longest to complete

Support team reported ~35% fewer campaign-related tickets. Specialists confirmed it was faster.
4

Self-service positioning doesn't match user reality

Evidence: Sales team reported customers chose BrightBid for the "human touch" of digital specialists

What I recommended: Shared research findings suggesting a shift toward an agency model might better serve user needs

Company moved toward agency model ~18 months later under new leadership

What I Took Away from BrightBid

1

Sometimes you have to prove an idea won't work

Leadership wanted a mobile app. Rather than disagreeing outright, I explored the idea through multiple design attempts. None of them worked well for the use case. Sometimes the most useful thing is to thoroughly explore an idea before deciding against it.

2

Research beats assumptions

We assumed users wanted self-service. They didn't—they wanted human support. This only became clear through direct conversations with users. The company later moved in this direction.

3

Trust in AI is earned, not assumed

Users won't trust AI just because it's there. You have to show them it's reliable, give them control over what it does, and always let them undo things. Trust builds slowly.

What didn't work

My first dashboard design was too ambitious. I designed an elaborate widget system with dozens of customization options—data density controls, color themes, complex filtering. Users were overwhelmed. In testing, they asked "can I just see my campaigns?" I stripped it back to the essentials: health scores, alerts, and three preset views. Simpler was better. The lesson: start minimal, add complexity only when users ask for it.
