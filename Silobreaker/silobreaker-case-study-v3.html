The Short Version
I joined Silobreaker as a Product Designer and within 8 months became the design system owner. I built Ymir, a design system based on Material 3, that brought consistency to a 20-year-old intelligence platform. The system doubled our design velocity (self-tracked), reached 100% team adoption, and scored 85% positive in a team survey. Along the way, I designed key features like a customizable nav rail, unified search, and dashboards v2 — and built an AI prototyping tool that the design team now uses as a shared resource.

How we got there

We evaluated several design system foundations—testing each direction against real Silobreaker pages and gathering feedback from 10-15 stakeholders. We landed on Material 3 as our foundation. I also added a level below atoms—"Quarks"—for typography and colors. Subatomic particles.

Key decisions

⚔ Going all-in (no legacy fallback)

Some stakeholders wanted to keep the old design system as a fallback. After discussion, the team concluded that maintaining two systems would slow us down. We committed fully to Ymir.

⚔ Building executive buy-in

The ROI of a design system wasn't immediately obvious to everyone. Rather than arguing the case in theory, I focused on showing prototypes that demonstrated the difference. With support from PMs and developers, the team built a shared understanding of the value.

📈 Re-engaging an at-risk customer

One client had been on the CS team's radar for over a year due to internal restructuring and declining engagement. After previewing features I designed — geofencing capabilities and dark mode on dashboards — their team re-engaged. Their threat intelligence manager began building a business case for expanded investment, saying "this shouldn't be hard to sell internally." When we showed dark mode, they said: "I was going to say geofencing was my new favourite feature, but then you mentioned this."

What didn't work

My first documentation approach failed. I created detailed Target Process pages that nobody read. Developers told me they wanted answers in context, not separate docs. So I pivoted to inline documentation in Figma—usage went up immediately. Lesson learned: meet people where they work.

02 / Features & Innovation

What I built beyond the design system

Each of these started as a distinct business problem. Click to explore.

Customizable Nav Rail
Surfaced hidden features, reduced clutter. Clients called it "really smooth" and "very sleek."

Problem: Our nav rail had ~10 items (guidelines say 5-7 max). One label was so long it forced the font size down.

Solution: A nav rail that expands on hover to reveal full labels. Users can customize which items appear vs. go to a "More items" panel. Smart defaults based on analytics.

Client feedback: "Really smooth to use, makes the UI look very sleek, and highlights features like Geolists and Webhooks that could have easily been missed before."

Expandable on hover Customizable Analytics-driven defaults
Unified Search
Replaced Notepad workarounds with an expandable search panel. 2 months, 12 users surveyed.

Problem: Power users were writing long queries but couldn't see the entire query. They resorted to Notepad.

Solution: A search panel (like Slack's Cmd+K) with growing query field, recent assets from all types, preview functionality, and a surfaced power-user feature: Fuzzy Asset Finder.

Client feedback: A long-time user said they loved that long queries now automatically expand the query box — a huge improvement over having to use Notepad to write complex queries.

~2 months of work. 12 end users surveyed. 5 CS team interviews.

Dashboards v2
Resizable widgets with live preview. A partner built dashboards with zero training. 2x faster setup.

Problem: Users couldn't customize widget sizes. No preview of settings. Placement was hard to control.

Solution: Resizable widgets with live preview, smart widget behavior (documents adjust based on size), edit mode, FAB for adding widgets.

A consulting partner built out operational and tactical dashboards with no training — and called it "the best in terms of widget development, clickability, and user-friendliness" across multiple CTI vendors they'd evaluated.

AI Prototyping Tool
Built a working React prototype using Claude Code + Figma MCP. ~60% faster prototyping, now a shared team tool.

Figma prototypes are useful, but they had limitations for intricate interactions. With a Claude Pro license, I explored building a working frontend prototype tool — a React implementation of Silobreaker's UI for testing concepts.

I used Claude Code and Figma MCP extensively. I could link frames to Claude and it would understand 80-90% of what I wanted. I even went frame-by-frame in Figma to illustrate interactions.

My design lead helped me set up the React project. I then shared what I'd learned with our other senior designer, and we now use it as a shared tool.

Learnings kept in mind moving forward

1

Show, don't tell

Arguments alone rarely convince. Prototypes that demonstrate the difference do. Seeing is believing.

2

Frame design as a business problem

"This will look better" doesn't land. "This will save developer time and reduce cost" does.

3

Build alliances across roles

Having PMs, developers, and the Product Director aligned made the difference when advocating for change.
