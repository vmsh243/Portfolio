The Short Version
I joined Zoniz as an intern with minimal experience. Two months in, I became the sole designer. For 22 months, I owned the design work — and for the last 6 months, I also took on interim PM responsibilities.

The platform had significant design debt: Adobe Illustrator handoffs, skeuomorphic visuals, accessibility gaps. The Journey

From inheriting a broken system to building something restaurants actually wanted to use. Here's what happened.

Months 1–2

The Inheritance

The existing designs came from a graphic design background—Adobe Illustrator handoffs, skeuomorphic visuals that weren't built for different screen sizes, and accessibility gaps throughout.

Two months after I started as an intern, the role became mine. Suddenly I was the only designer for a platform used by dozens of restaurants.

My job was to clean it up and make it actually work. For the next 22 months, I was the only designer—and for the last 6 months, I also took on interim PM responsibilities.

Months 3–6

The 35% Error Rate Crisis

35% of orders had the same issue: users accidentally asked for the check when they meant to order more food. They'd tap through the flow, not realize their order was sent, and then panic when the bill showed up.

I ran observational research at DOT (a local restaurant who became our champion and closest collaborator for testing and research), watching customers use the app. The confusion was obvious once I saw it happen in real time—people genuinely didn't know what they'd just done.

But the bigger problem? Most customers were annoyed they had to download an app just to order food. My redesign focused on two things: making the flow forgiving of mistakes (moved "Order more" to the right as primary action), and confirming before sending. Then I tackled the app requirement itself—I argued for a lightweight web experience that would open when scanning the QR code.

Months 6–12

I Worked as a Waiter for a Day

I talked to about 60 restaurant staff across 15 restaurants. But the real insight came when I worked as a waiter at one of our premium rooftop bar clients. Using the app I designed, in the middle of a dinner rush, showed me problems I never would've caught in a usability test.

In bright Romanian summer sun, the screen was unreadable. I tested this myself by walking onto the office balcony and at restaurant terraces with a mid-range phone—the kind waiters actually use.

The existing waiter app was a mess: contrast issues, confusing notifications, slow ordering flow, and no supervisor view. I addressed every one of these issues with high contrast UI, color-coded notification states, reduced taps with larger touch targets, and table layout visualization.

An Unexpected Accessibility Win

Restaurant owners told me their multilingual staff—some of whom were still learning Romanian and English—could use the app effectively and serve food to the right tables without issues. The owners credited the clear iconography. That wasn't something I explicitly designed for, but it validated that the design transcended language barriers.

Months 12–18

When Prototypes Fail in Real Life

Restaurant supervisors needed a bigger view to manage orders across all tables. My first attempt? I just upscaled the phone UI. In the Figma prototype, it looked fine.

In a real restaurant during dinner service? Complete disaster. The upscaled UI was too slow for busy staff. We went through 5 iterations in 3 months.

I redesigned it with horizontally scrolling product grids, quick print options right on the cart panel, product categories in a scrollable row, and table layout visualization. Users appreciated the predictability—products stayed in the same spot, so they could learn the layout and get there fast.

Months 18–22

CMS Dashboard & Expansion

Restaurant owners needed to understand their business performance, but the data was scattered and hard to parse. I ran card sorting exercises to figure out which statistics mattered most: total sales by payment method, sales per POS, sales by waiter, and most sold products.

Then I added shortcuts to the most commonly used features right on the dashboard. Owners loved checking in multiple times a day to get a health check on sales. The most-used shortcuts were "Products sold this month" and "Today's reservations."

Strategic Decisions

Key Design Decisions

Bottom Sheet Cart

I proposed using a bottom sheet as the shopping cart metaphor. It took 2 months of iteration and discussion with the team to refine the approach. The pattern helped reduce accidental bill requests.

Webapp Over App-Only

Stakeholders wanted to keep everything in the native app. Research showed customers were reluctant to download an app just to order food, so I advocated for a parallel web experience. The webapp saw roughly 4x the adoption of the native app.

Saying No to Full Migration

After the webapp succeeded, stakeholders wanted to move everything to web. I shared concerns: the app included touchpoints that nudged users toward loyalty programs, special products, and deeper engagement. Removing those risked losing the path to long-term retention.

Mentorship

The Cook Who Became a Designer

From Kitchen to Design

One of the restaurants that used Zoniz had a cook who'd taken a UX design course. He reached out, curious about the work. I started mentoring him—and his perspective as someone who actually worked in a kitchen was invaluable.

We ended up designing a lot of the kitchen UI together. He knew things I never would have thought to ask about: where tablets get mounted, how greasy hands interact with screens, what information you need at a glance when orders are backing up.

What I Learned

1

Prototype fidelity matters—but real-world testing matters more

Even perfect Figma prototypes can't predict everything. The tablet interface looked great in Figma and failed in a real restaurant. I had to learn to test in context, not just in the design tool.

2

Working as a user is invaluable

I learned more in one dinner service as a waiter than I did in 10 interviews. Nothing beats experiencing your own design in the wild.

3

Mistakes are the best teacher

I made a lot of them. Being the only designer meant there was no one to catch things before they shipped. That was stressful, but each mistake taught me something I still carry with me.

What didn't work

Early on, I designed the staff app with too many customization options—thinking flexibility was always good. Restaurants got confused by settings they didn't understand. Some configured things incorrectly and blamed the app when orders went wrong. I stripped out most options and made smart defaults instead. Sometimes the best UX is fewer choices, not more.
